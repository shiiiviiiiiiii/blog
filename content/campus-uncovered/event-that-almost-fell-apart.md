---
title: "Notes from Organizing an Event That Almost Fell Apart"
date: "2026-04-18"
excerpt: "What three days of crisis taught me about leadership that no leadership workshop ever did."
---

Three days before our fest's main event, our headline act cancelled.

I was part of the core organizing team, and I remember the exact moment the message came in — a group chat that had been quiet for an hour suddenly had eleven people typing at once.

What I want to write about isn't the scramble that followed, though that's a story too. What I want to write about is what I noticed about people during those three days.

The person who'd been loudest in planning meetings — full of ideas, quick with opinions — went quiet. Not in a bad way. I think the scale of the problem was new to them, and they needed a moment to recalibrate.

The person who'd said the least in meetings turned out to have a contact who could get us a replacement act within 24 hours. They'd been sitting on this connection the whole time, I think, because no one had asked the right question.

And the person nominally "in charge" — our event lead — did something I didn't expect. They didn't take over. They asked each of us, individually, "what's the one thing you think you're best positioned to do right now?" And then let us answer.

It wasn't dramatic. No one gave a speech. But within an hour, five different threads were moving — sponsorship calls, the replacement act, a backup plan if that fell through, communications to the audience, and a quiet recheck of the budget to see what we could still afford.

The event happened. It wasn't the event we'd planned, but it happened, and most people in the audience never knew anything had gone wrong.

What I took from this: the loudest person in a calm room and the most useful person in a crisis are often not the same person. And good leadership, at least in that moment, looked less like decisiveness and more like asking the right question and then getting out of the way.

I don't think you can simulate this in a workshop. You can only really learn it by being in the room when something breaks.
