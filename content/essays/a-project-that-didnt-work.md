---
title: "A Project That Didn't Work"
date: "2026-02-19"
excerpt: "I spent four months building something nobody used. Here's what I keep coming back to."
---

I built something last year that almost no one used.

I won't go into the specifics — not because I'm protecting anyone, but because the specifics don't matter. What matters is the shape of it: an idea I was excited about, four months of work, a launch that landed quietly, and then nothing. A few polite messages. A slow fade into irrelevance.

For a while, I treated this as a verdict. The idea was bad. I was bad at judging ideas. Maybe I should stick to things I'm already good at.

But the more time passes, the less that framing feels true.

What actually happened is simpler and less dramatic: I built something for a problem I had, assumed other people had the same problem in the same way, and never really checked. I was so focused on building that I skipped the part where you find out if anyone wants the thing before you finish it.

That's not a verdict on me. That's a specific, fixable mistake. The kind you can only really learn by making it.

I think there's a temptation, after something doesn't work, to extract a moral that's bigger than the situation deserves. "I'm not cut out for this." "This isn't my path." Big, identity-level conclusions drawn from medium-sized data points.

I'm trying to resist that temptation now. Not by pretending the failure didn't sting — it did — but by being precise about what it actually taught me. It taught me to talk to people earlier. It taught me that I enjoy the building more than I enjoy the validating, and that I need to balance those instincts. It taught me that four months is a long time to spend without checking your assumptions.

That's it. That's the lesson. Not "give up." Just: check earlier next time.

I think most failures are like this — smaller and more specific than they feel in the moment. The work is learning to see them at their actual size.
