---
title: "What Theatre Taught Me About Listening"
date: "2026-04-02"
excerpt: "Before I learned to act, I had to learn to listen — really listen, not just wait for my line."
---

My first director told me something I didn't understand until much later: "Your best acting happens when you're not speaking."

At the time, I thought this was a riddle. I was twenty, eager, and convinced that the value I brought to a scene was in my lines — the words I'd rehearsed, the inflections I'd practiced in the mirror. Listening felt like a passive activity. Something you did between the parts that mattered.

I was wrong about almost all of it.

The actors who moved me most weren't the ones who delivered lines well. They were the ones who reacted like the words were happening to them for the first time, every single night. They were listening — actually listening — to their scene partner, and letting that listening change something in their face, their posture, the timing of their next breath.

It took me two years of being on stage to understand that listening is not the gap between talking. Listening is the work.

I think about this constantly now, off stage. In conversations, in meetings, in arguments. How much of what passes for communication is really just two people taking turns broadcasting? How rarely do we let what someone says actually land before we respond?

Theatre gave me a strange gift: it made me suspicious of my own eagerness to speak. Now, when I notice myself forming a response before the other person has finished their sentence, I try to catch it. Not always successfully. But more than I used to.

People rarely remember what you said. They remember whether you were actually there with them while they said it.
